My Story

At the end of 2023, my startup shuttered its doors and I didn't have a plan for what was next. The startup, based in the US, sponsored my work visa, which became void as soon as the company ceased to exist. So my immediate priority was to secure another US visa, otherwise I would have to uproot my life in the bay area. At that point, I was addicted to the founder life and I really didn't want to return to being an employee. I decided to apply for an O-1A visa. It's reserved for individuals of “extraordinary ability” with certain standards of excellence you must meet to qualify. I was intimidated. And I was not confident about my chances. But it was the best path that supported self-petitioning while not having a clear idea of my next business venture. So within the span of a few months, I raced to mount the best case I could, gathering as much evidence as possible to prove I was “extraordinary”. Thankfully, in the spring of 2024, the effort paid off and I received my O-1A visa.

Since then, I've been coaching other immigrants working in tech through career transitions, with a specialty in preparing and applying for an O-1A visa.

FAQ

What's the O-1A visa?

The O-1A visa is for individuals who can show that they have extraordinary ability in the sciences, education or business. For the US government, this means having "a level of expertise indicating that the person is one of the small percentage who have risen to the very top of the field of endeavor." (As a rule of thumb, you're likely eligible if you're in the 90th percentile of your domain.) For the US immigration officers who review applications, this means providing evidence of "sustained national or international acclaim and recognition for achievements in the field of expertise."

Practically speaking, in order to obtain an O-1A visa, you will need to prove that you meet at least three of the following eight criteria:

  1. High Salary: Command of a high salary or remuneration relative to others in the field.
  2. Awards/Prizes: Receipt of nationally or internationally recognized prizes/awards for excellence.
  3. Membership: Membership in professional associations requiring outstanding achievements for entry.
  4. Publications: Published material about you in professional/major trade publications or media.
  5. Judging: Participation as a judge of others' work in the same or similar field.
  6. Original Contributions: Original scientific, scholarly, or business-related contributions of major significance.
  7. Scholarly Articles: Authorship of scholarly articles in professional journals or major media.
  8. Critical Employment Capacity: Employment in a critical or essential capacity at distinguished organizations.

However, individuals who have won (or in some cases been nominated for) a major international award of the caliber of the Nobel Prize are able to rely on that award alone to obtain an O-1A visa. (I'm going to assume this doesn't apply to you.)

Although the criteria seems indimidating (it did for me), satisfying many of them is actually more feasible than you think (C'mon, I was able to do it).

The best part about the O-1A visa is that you don't need employer sponsorship--You can self-sponsor. That's why it's a solution for many immigrants in STEM or business who want to found a startup, build a lifestyle business, or simply take a career break to explore professionally.

How are you different from a lawyer?

Let me state that I'm NOT a substitute for an immigration attorney. Nothing I say should ever be constituted as legal advice.

When you hire an immigration lawyer, they handle the legal aspects of your application, such as drafting and filing your petition, interpreting immigration laws, and ensuring compliance with USCIS requirements. Attorneys are essential for the formal legal process.

Me, on the other hand, I focus on helping you navigate the areas where attorneys typically don't offer assistance. This includes:

  • Offering career coaching, well before you're ready to engage with an immigration firm, to enhance your eligibility for the O-1A visa.
  • Helping you gather evidence, such as identifying awards, memberships, publications, or judging opportunities that strengthen your case. It's hard to accomplish any of this in a few months while working with a lawyer.
  • Guiding you on how to present your achievements effectively to attorneys or in your petition.
  • Bridging the gap between your technical expertise and the legal nuances of the visa application process.

In essence, I help you prepare and strategize before you start the application process, as far as 1 year out. Think of me as a guide for the practical and tactical aspects leading up to an application, while your attorney handles the application itself.
